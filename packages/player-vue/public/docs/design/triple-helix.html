"Three intertwined streams of content, experienced in rotation, creating natural spacing without complex algorithms."

The Triple Helix is a content distribution system that divides learning material into three parallel "tubes" (or threads). The learner rotates between tubes, ensuring that by the time they return to any given tube, time has passed and spacing has occurred naturally.

Seeds are distributed to tubes like dealing cards:

Formula: tube = (seed_number - 1) % 3
  Seed 1: (1-1) % 3 = 0 â†’ Tube A
  Seed 2: (2-1) % 3 = 1 â†’ Tube B
  Seed 3: (3-1) % 3 = 2 â†’ Tube C
  Seed 4: (4-1) % 3 = 0 â†’ Tube A

Why "Card Dealing"?

  • Even distribution: Each tube gets roughly equal content
  • Deterministic: Same seed always maps to same tube
  • Simple: No complex assignment algorithms
  • Progressive: Tubes advance together through the course

Natural Spacing

When you practice SEED 1 in Tube A, then rotate through B and C, approximately 5-7 minutes pass before you return to Tube A. This creates natural spaced repetition without explicit scheduling.

The Spacing Window

Each Round takes ~2-3 minutes. Rotating through 3 tubes means ~5-7 minutes between tube visits. This is optimal for short-term consolidation within a session.

âœ¨ Why Three Tubes?

1. Cognitive Reset

Switching tubes forces a context switch. The brain "sets aside" Tube A content while working on Tube B, allowing background consolidation.

2. Variety Without Chaos

Three tubes provide enough variety to prevent monotony while remaining manageable. Two would be too binary; four or more would fragment attention too much.

3. Failure Isolation

If a learner struggles in Tube A, they get a break in Tubes B and C. Frustration doesn't compound continuously.

4. Progress Perception

The learner sees three "tracks" of progress. This feels more substantial than a single linear path.

The Triple Helix Effect

Like strands of DNA intertwined, the three tubes weave together to create a learning experience that is varied, spaced, and cognitively sustainable.

When to Collapse

  • Content injection: Need to introduce vocabulary outside the normal sequence
  • Catch-up: Learner fell behind in one tube
  • Mastery acceleration: One tube content is fully consolidated

Collapse is Rare

In normal operation, all three tubes remain active. Collapse is an edge-case mechanism for adaptive scenarios.
